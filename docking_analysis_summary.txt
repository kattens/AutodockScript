3. Cases Where Non-native Ligands Score Better
   - Non-native ligand outperformed the native ligand in 3 out of 13 cases (23.1%):
         1D7O_TCL_Q9BJJ9

   - These cases are expected due to limitations of scoring functions:
         • empirical scoring function simplifications
         • hydrophobic or large ligands artificially over-scoring
         • no solvation/entropy effects
         • docking into incorrect pockets

4. Statistical Test
   Wilcoxon paired signed-rank test:
       statistic = 21.0
       p-value   = 0.176
   Interpretation:
       • Does not reach p < 0.05 significance.
       • Expected due to small sample size (n = 13).
       • However, native still trends lower overall.

5. Effect Size (Qualitative Interpretation)
   - The difference (~1 kcal/mol) corresponds to a medium biochemical effect size.
   - Separation of medians supports the hypothesis:
         Native interactions are stronger and more reliable.

6. Visualization (from boxplot)
   - Native ligands show lower median energy (~ -10.0)
   - Non-native ligands show higher median (~ -8.6)
   - Overlap exists (normal in docking)
   - Trend is still correct: native binds stronger.

7. Practical Scientific Interpretation
   - Docking scores alone cannot perfectly classify native vs non-native ligands.
   - Only ~23% of cases show score inversion.
   - These outliers will typically show:
         • incorrect binding pocket
         • wrong pose orientation
         • unrealistic contacts
         • high RMSD to native binding mode
   - 3D structural validation is required to interpret these exceptions.

8. Conclusion
   - Native ligands show stronger binding on average.
   - Non-native ligands tend to bind weaker but occasionally over-score due to scoring function limitations.
   - Combined affinity + 3D pose validation provides strong evidence that:
         • Native docking is biologically correct
         • Non-native strong-scoring cases are false positives from scoring approximations.
